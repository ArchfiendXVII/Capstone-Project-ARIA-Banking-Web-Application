### 1.2 Consultant executive narrative

ARIA Bank’s current web application security posture shows critical access-control weaknesses alongside foundational hardening gaps. The highest business risk is unauthorized access to other customers’ data and functions, which can directly translate into privacy breaches, regulatory impact, customer harm, and reputational damage. Key issues include broken access control around dashboards (F-03) and transaction history exposure (F-04), plus authentication hardening gaps that increase the likelihood of credential-stuffing and account compromise (F-01, F-02).

From a business-impact perspective, the combination of IDOR weaknesses (F-03, F-04) and missing/insufficient defensive controls (F-01, F-02) creates a clear path for attackers to enumerate or view other customers’ information without authorization. While some privilege-related controls appear to be working as intended (F-05, profile role tampering), overall risk remains elevated due to high-impact failures in core user authorization paths and transactional/data-handling areas.

Top 3 priorities for ARIA leadership are: (1) immediately remediate the broken access control findings (F-03, F-04) to prevent cross-user data exposure; (2) harden authentication and session/secret management (F-01, F-02, F-16) to reduce account takeover likelihood; and (3) address high-risk database and application safety issues (F-09, F-13, F-14) to prevent both direct exploitation (e.g., injection) and indirect exposure through excessive data and insufficient auditability.
### 1.1 Business impact (plain language)

ARIA Bank handles customer accounts, transfers, and personal data. Findings marked **Non-Compliant** represent concrete paths an attacker or unauthorized user could abuse — including access to other customers' data, privilege escalation, or incomplete audit trails that would hinder incident response. This report is intended for security and engineering leadership to prioritize remediation.

### 1.3 Overall risk narrative

Overall, ARIA Bank’s application posture indicates a “high likelihood, high impact” pattern common to early-stage or rapidly evolving systems: access control controls are not consistently enforced at the data retrieval layer (e.g., IDOR in F-03/F-04), authentication protections are incomplete (F-02) and secrets appear to be improperly managed (F-01/F-16), and several safety controls around data handling and audit logging need strengthening (F-09, F-13, F-14). While select authorization and privilege-management paths are behaving correctly (notably F-05), the current gaps create an attacker-friendly environment for both account compromise and unauthorized data access.
