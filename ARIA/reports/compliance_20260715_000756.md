### 1.2 Consultant executive narrative

ARIA Bank’s current application security posture shows significant access control weaknesses that directly elevate the risk of unauthorized data access and fraud. The most material findings are user-to-user data exposure via Insecure Direct Object References (IDOR) (F-03, F-04), plus account takeover support from missing or inadequate authentication protections (F-02) and insecure credential handling (F-01). Collectively, these issues create a realistic pathway for an attacker to view other customers’ information and potentially disrupt transactions.

Business impact includes regulatory and customer harm risk: exposing customer-specific dashboard and transaction history data can trigger privacy and contractual violations, increase incident response costs, and damage customer trust. Even where some controls pass (notably profile role tampering, F-05; and improvements for transfers are partially present in F-18 where checks pass), the overall risk remains High due to the combination of authorization failures (IDOR) and authentication gaps. From a security operations perspective, incomplete login failure logging (partially indicated within F-02) also reduces the bank’s ability to detect and investigate account attacks.

Top 3 priorities are: (1) fix all authorization flaws causing IDOR (F-03, F-04, and any related excessive exposure in F-13), (2) harden authentication and session/secret foundations (F-02, F-01, F-16), and (3) correct backend data handling issues that enable injection or unsafe queries (F-09, plus supporting logging hardening in F-11 and CSRF in F-08). These actions reduce the likelihood of both data breaches and account takeover quickly while improving investigatability and audit readiness.
### 1.1 Business impact (plain language)

ARIA Bank handles customer accounts, transfers, and personal data. Findings marked **Non-Compliant** represent concrete paths an attacker or unauthorized user could abuse — including access to other customers' data, privilege escalation, or incomplete audit trails that would hinder incident response. This report is intended for security and engineering leadership to prioritize remediation.

### 1.3 Overall risk narrative

Overall, ARIA Bank’s posture is best characterized as “target-rich, exploit-ready” on customer data exposure and authentication hardening. The application appears to have correct defenses in some areas (e.g., profile role tampering control F-05 is compliant), but critical access control tests indicate that authorization decisions are not consistently bound to the authenticated user. In parallel, several foundational security controls around secrets/password handling, login protection, and query safety remain non-compliant or incomplete, which increases the probability that a motivated attacker can progress from reconnaissance to unauthorized access. The findings also show an opportunity to strengthen privacy-by-design and transfer robustness (F-17 and F-18) to reduce regulatory exposure and transaction integrity risk.
