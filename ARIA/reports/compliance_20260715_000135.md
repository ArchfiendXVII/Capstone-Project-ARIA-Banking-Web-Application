### 1.2 Consultant executive narrative

ARIA Bank’s current application security and privacy control coverage shows material weaknesses in account protection, access control, and data handling. The most significant risks are centered on broken access control patterns (IDOR in F-03 and F-04), weak authentication resilience (F-02), and unsafe credential/secret handling and database query practices (F-01 and F-09). These issues collectively increase the likelihood of unauthorized data access, account takeover attempts, and potential compromise of customer information.

The business impact is high: IDOR defects can allow an attacker to view other customers’ dashboard and transaction history data, which can translate into direct confidentiality exposure and regulatory consequences. Weak login protections and incomplete audit logging (observed in F-02) reduce the bank’s ability to detect and respond to brute-force attempts. Additionally, secret weaknesses and SQL injection exposure risk can lead to systemic impact across services and data stores.

Top 3 priorities are: (1) immediately remediate broken access control (F-03, F-04, and role/customer portal boundaries F-06/F-07), (2) harden authentication and session/secret management (F-02, F-01/F-16), and (3) close injection and data exposure gaps (F-09, F-13, and ensure audit logging completeness F-14). Addressing these first materially reduces both likelihood and impact of a security event.
### 1.1 Business impact (plain language)

ARIA Bank handles customer accounts, transfers, and personal data. Findings marked **Non-Compliant** represent concrete paths an attacker or unauthorized user could abuse — including access to other customers' data, privilege escalation, or incomplete audit trails that would hinder incident response. This report is intended for security and engineering leadership to prioritize remediation.

### 1.3 Overall risk narrative

ARIA Bank’s overall security posture is mixed: several controls appear effective (e.g., profile role tampering in F-05 is compliant, and customer vs. employee/admin portal boundaries appear partially addressed), but there are critical and high-risk failures that would likely be exploitable by a motivated attacker. The presence of IDOR findings (F-03/F-04) indicates insufficient object-level authorization enforcement, while authentication controls (F-02) and sensitive data protections (F-01, F-13) show clear gaps. Injection risk (F-09) further elevates systemic risk by potentially enabling unauthorized database operations; while some transfer design weaknesses are present (F-18), that area should be treated as an important follow-on to prevent financial integrity and reconciliation issues.
