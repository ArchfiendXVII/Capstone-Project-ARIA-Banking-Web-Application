### 1.2 Consultant executive narrative

ARIA Bank’s current application security posture shows several high-impact weaknesses that could allow unauthorized access to customer data and account actions. The most material risk drivers are access control failures (notably IDOR in F-03 and F-04), and authentication hardening gaps (F-02 and F-01). Collectively, these issues increase the likelihood of sensitive data exposure, fraud, and reputational harm—particularly because banking applications must assume motivated adversaries.

Business impact is significant: IDOR findings can enable customers (or external attackers) to view other users’ dashboards and transaction history, potentially triggering regulatory reporting and customer remediation. Authentication weaknesses (missing/ineffective throttling and weak secret handling) can accelerate account takeover attempts, while incomplete logging (notably in the login failure pathway under F-02) can delay detection and investigation. Even where some controls pass (e.g., profile role tampering in F-05 and customer blocking for employee portal in F-06), the remaining non-compliant items still represent a credible path to compromise.

Top priorities should be: (1) immediately remediate access control flaws causing IDOR (F-03, F-04, and customer/admin boundary in F-07 where flagged), (2) harden authentication and session/security secrets (F-02 and F-01), and (3) address injection and data exposure risks (F-09 and F-13). In parallel, strengthen transfer design robustness (F-18) and privacy workflows (F-17) to reduce the likelihood of financial/account integrity issues and regulatory exposure.
### 1.1 Business impact (plain language)

ARIA Bank handles customer accounts, transfers, and personal data. Findings marked **Non-Compliant** represent concrete paths an attacker or unauthorized user could abuse — including access to other customers' data, privilege escalation, or incomplete audit trails that would hinder incident response. This report is intended for security and engineering leadership to prioritize remediation.

### 1.3 Overall risk narrative

Overall, ARIA Bank’s posture reflects an uneven control implementation: several critical-to-high access control and authentication/injection safeguards are not meeting expected baselines, while a few important authorization controls are correctly implemented (e.g., F-05 role tampering is passing and F-06 blocks customers from the employee portal). This combination suggests that an attacker could first gain unauthorized visibility (IDOR) and/or accelerate authentication attempts (F-02) and then potentially expand impact via unsafe backend handling (F-09) and excessive data exposure (F-13). On the positive side, control areas related to some admin/profile protections appear resilient, and transaction transfer improvements for F-18 are partially addressed via checks that pass—however, remaining transfer design gaps still warrant prompt remediation.
